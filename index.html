While Paragraph 14(A)(6) does address the discharge of firearms, the clause specificaly addresses a resident's prohibition of engaging in such conduct, and implies a restriction on the resident engaging in intentional conduct in violation of that paragraph. I've attached the citation referenced above which again reflects that my client was not the individual involved in the conduct or charged, and that >friend< was charged with negligent conduct, not intentional conduct. In this regard, I suggest that a common sensical approach must be taken, which is consistent with South Carolina law. South Carolina Courts have plainly outlined that "the landlord's right to terminate is not unlimited and that the court's decision to permit termination must be tempered by notions of equity and common sense." Kiriakides v. United Artists Commc'ns, Inc., 312 S.C. 271, 276, 440 S.E.2d 364, 366 (1994). "Accordingly, we hold that a forfeiture for a trivial or immaterial breach of a commercial lease should not be enforced." Id. Equity and common sense will dictate that my client did not intentionally or willfully breach any lease provisions. South Carolina's "common sensical" approach and requirement of fundamental fairness is further outlined in the obligation of good faith in the enforcement of any rights as codified in South Carolina Code Annotated Section 27-40-220. Beyond the foregoing, however, South Carolina Code Annotated Section 27-40- 710 establishes an obligation on the landlord to provide tenants with an opportunity to cure any alleged default, assuming a default has been demonstrated. Any attempts to have tenants waive this right is unenforceable. See South Carolina Code Annotated Section 27- 40-330(a)(1). These provisions are designed to ensure that accidental or unintentional "violations" or "defaults" do not give rise to a loss of possession, as they are allowed to be cured. As outlined above, as soon as the accidental discharge occurred, my client undertook steps to ensure 911 was contacted and that no one had been injured. My client addressed the situation responsibly and immediately. My client is a good tenant and has every intention of complying with every term and condition of the lease, as he always has. He enjoys the property and does not want to get into a legal dispute, but as noted herein, he stands on solid legal ground. I am happy to discuss any of the foregoing with your property management team or legal counsel. For these legal reasons, please understand that my client disagrees with your suggestion that he violated any term of his lease and has no intention of vacating the premises. I thank you for taking the time to review the aforementioned legal authorities. After doing so, I'm sure that you will come to the same conclusion that I have and will allow my client to remain in possession of the space and will not pursue eviction as it is unwarranted.
